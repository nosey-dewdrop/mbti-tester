what is mbti?

the actual science behind the 4 letters

1921

it starts with carl jung

swiss psychiatrist carl gustav jung published Psychological Types in 1921. he proposed that human behavior isn't random — people have innate preferences for how they perceive the world and make decisions.

jung identified 8 cognitive functions — mental processes that every person uses, but in different orders and intensities. your dominant function shapes how you experience reality.

this wasn't pop psychology. this was clinical observation across thousands of patients.

1943

briggs & myers build the indicator

katharine cook briggs and her daughter isabel briggs myers took jung's theory and made it practical. during world war II, they developed the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) — a questionnaire to help women entering the workforce find jobs that matched their cognitive style.

they organized jung's functions into 4 dichotomies — pairs of opposing preferences. each person leans toward one side of each pair, creating 16 possible combinations.

the MBTI became the most widely used personality assessment in the world. over 2 million people take it annually.

the 4 dichotomies

Extraversion (E) vs Introversion (I) — where you get energy. not about being shy or loud — it's about whether social interaction charges you up or drains your battery.

Sensing (S) vs iNtuition (N) — how you take in information. sensors focus on concrete data, what's real and present. intuitives focus on patterns, possibilities, and what could be.

Thinking (T) vs Feeling (F) — how you make decisions. thinkers prioritize logic and consistency. feelers prioritize values and impact on people. both are rational — just different criteria.

Judging (J) vs Perceiving (P) — how you structure your life. judgers want closure, plans, and order. perceivers want flexibility, spontaneity, and options.

cognitive function stacks

dominant — your core
auxiliary — your support
tertiary — developing
inferior — your blind spot

here's where it gets deep. each type doesn't just have 4 letters — they have a function stack. a hierarchy of 4 cognitive functions that determines how they actually process reality.

the 8 functions:

Se extraverted sensing — lives in the moment, physical awareness
Si introverted sensing — memory, tradition, detailed recall
Ne extraverted intuition — possibilities, connections, what-ifs
Ni introverted intuition — vision, foresight, "just knowing"
Te extraverted thinking — efficiency, systems, organizing the world
Ti introverted thinking — internal logic, frameworks, analysis
Fe extraverted feeling — group harmony, reading the room
Fi introverted feeling — personal values, authenticity, deep empathy

for example, an INFP (me) has: Fi → Ne → Si → Te. introverted feeling leads — everything is filtered through deep personal values. extraverted intuition supports — we see possibilities everywhere. our weakest function is Te (extraverted thinking) — which is why we're terrible at organizing and efficiency.

an INTJ has: Ni → Te → Fi → Se. introverted intuition leads — they see the future and plan for it. supported by extraverted thinking — ruthless efficiency. that's why they're strategic masterminds. and why i don't trust them.

the criticism (and why i still use it)

let's be honest: mbti gets criticism from academic psychology. and some of it is valid.

test-retest reliability: up to 50% of people get a different result when retaking the test. but this is the test's problem, not the theory's. the 4-letter system is a simplification of jung's continuous model. you're not "an INFP" — you lean INFP.

the barnum effect: descriptions are vague enough to apply to anyone. true for bad descriptions (looking at you, 16personalities). the cognitive function model is much more specific and falsifiable.

big five is "better": the OCEAN model (openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, neuroticism) has stronger empirical support. but mbti and big five measure different things. mbti maps cognitive preferences — how you think. big five maps behavioral traits — how you act.

i use mbti not as gospel, but as a framework for observation. it gives you vocabulary to describe patterns you already notice in people. and when you're sitting across from someone on a date trying to figure out if they're going to ghost you or marry you — vocabulary helps.

how people take in the world and make decisions

combine all four and you get one of 16 types. an INTJ is introverted, intuitive, thinking, judging — the strategic mastermind who will sacrifice you for the greater good. an ESFP is extraverted, sensing, feeling, perceiving — the life of the party who lives entirely in the moment.
